# Xiaotiancai Takes Down Tutorials and Bans Accounts

> This article contains content that has been **passed around multiple times** — not everything may be accurate. If there are errors, feel free to open an Issue.

On January 26, 2026, Xiaotiancai took down various tutorial videos online.

- Removed a large number of tutorial videos from Bilibili, Douyin, and other platforms.
- Banned three Bilibili accounts for **30 days**.
- **Permanently** banned at least one Douyin account.

They also sent a **communication letter** (content is a cloned version, not the official version, since the official one can't be verified): ![Image](../../assets/371cecf6f4dede531f8497c33b2eaba7.png)

> To Xiaotiancai crackers:
> We did nothing wrong.
> Publishing root tutorials was our own hobby, but Xiaotiancai claims it will cause minors to "become addicted" and harm physical and mental health.
> The sense of achievement from rooting — only we understand.

As shown in the image, Xiaotiancai used "anti-addiction" as the reason, along with the so-called laughable "infringement" (Xiaotiancai itself doesn't comply with open source agreements, and some videos just teach how to install third-party apps), to send the notice.

It mentioned three points:

- Inducing downloads of unreviewed games, violating anti-addiction policy
- Involving infringement (but didn't mention the **injured party**)
- Violating the "Qinglang" rectification campaign

First, **rooting** (what they call **cracking**) is not a game itself — the manufacturer is just grasping at straws. Will rooting make people addicted? Obviously not (or at least not typically).
Second, is modifying our own watches wrong? Publishing root tutorials is an educational video, and not everyone installs games from them.
