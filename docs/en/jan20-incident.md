# January 20 Incident: Friends Circle Overhaul

> On January 20, 2026, Xiaotiancai made a series of changes to the Friends Circle feature without any notice. Users had no warning — they woke up to find everything changed.

This wasn't bug fixing. This was cutting existing features that users already had. And it was done without users' knowledge or consent.

---

## Change 1: Friends Circle Like Visibility Reduced

**Before:** You could see anyone's likes in Friends Circle. Even if you weren't friends with the poster and didn't know the person who liked it, the like still showed up.

**After:** You can only see likes from **mutual friends**. Likes from non-mutual friends are hidden entirely.

On the surface, this is about "privacy protection." In reality, it's neutering a feature that was open and social by nature.

What's the point of Friends Circle? It's so friends can see each other's interactions. Hide likes from non-mutual friends, and Friends Circle loses most of its reason to exist.

**And there's no choice.** No toggle, no setting, no option for "do you want non-friends to see likes?" One-size-fits-all, just changed.

---

## Change 2: Likes Disappear When You Unfriend Someone

**Before:** You post something, people like it. Even if you later unfriend someone, their likes stay.

**After:** Unfriend someone, and all the likes they ever gave you are removed.

This change hurts most for kids who like collecting likes. You might have spent months building up hundreds of likes, then you fall out with someone and unfriend them — poof, all gone.

This isn't a technical limitation. It's deliberately designed this way.

Think about it: **unfriending = deleting all historical interaction records.** No social product — WeChat, QQ, Instagram — does this. Because likes are ancillary data to the content itself, not to the "friend relationship."

Why did Xiaotiancai change this? The only reasonable explanation is: **to discourage users from unfriending, maintaining friend relationship "stickiness."** Or they just didn't think it through, deciding "if you're not friends, it shouldn't show."

Either way, the user bears the consequences.

---

## Change 3: Benefit Cards Renamed to "Genius PIN," Gifting Removed

**Before:** Users could earn benefit cards through various activities and gift them to friends.

**After:** Benefit cards renamed to "Genius PIN," gifting no longer supported.

Benefit cards are essentially something users earned with their time or effort. Being able to gift them to friends represents a degree of control over one's own "assets." Removing gifting outright is the company saying:

**"What you earned — we decide how you use it."**

The rename itself isn't a big deal — "Genius PIN" isn't any better — but using the rename as an excuse to kill gifting is pretty dirty.

---

## Change 4: Level System Permanently Removed

Xiaotiancai watches used to have a **level system**. Users leveled up through daily use, interaction, and completing tasks. Levels weren't just a status symbol — they came with certain benefits and a sense of achievement.

For kids who grew up using Xiaotiancai watches, levels were a testament to their journey. Going from LV.1 up to higher levels — this process may have spanned several years.

Then Xiaotiancai just removed it.

**Permanently removed. Not paused, not revamped — straight up cut.**

All the levels you spent years accumulating — gone overnight. No compensation, no replacement.

"A generation's childhood just ended" — that's not an exaggeration.

For kids born after 2010 and 2015, Xiaotiancai's levels, Friends Circle, and likes are their "QQ Space." Try going back and zeroing out all the QQ levels of the post-80s and post-90s generations. See what happens.

But because it's kids' stuff, adults think "it's just a level system," so cut it be cut, and nobody speaks up for them.

---

## Change 5: Likes Replaced with "Sports Encouragement"

**Before:** Friends Circle had a like function, similar to WeChat Moments. You post something, friends can like it — the most basic social interaction.

**After:** The like function on the main page was cut, replaced with something called "Sports Encouragement."

Sports Encouragement basically means you can only like sports-related posts. Want to like a friend's everyday post? Not possible. Only "encouragement" for sports is allowed now.

The logic here is very clear: **Xiaotiancai wants to turn Friends Circle from a "social platform" into a "workout check-in platform."**

They think kids should use the watch for exercise and health, not socializing. So they cut the social features and replaced them with "Sports Encouragement."

But here's the thing — **this isn't what parents want, and it isn't what kids want. It's just what some product manager pulled out of thin air.**

Kids use the watch to interact with classmates — that's a social need. Trying to force a social product into a health product pleases no one: the social aspect is gone, and nobody uses the sports features either.

And like the changes above: **no notice before the change, no options after.**

---

## The Core Problem: No User Consent

Each of the changes above might seem "not that bad" individually. But put them together:

1. Like visibility reduced
2. Unfriending deletes likes
3. Benefit card gifting removed

**None of these changes were communicated to users in advance. None gave users a choice. None allowed users to keep the original functionality.**

Xiaotiancai's official explanation is "optimized based on feedback from some parents."

Some parents.

In other words, how your product changes depends entirely on "who yells the loudest." A group of parents say "I don't want my kids to see likes from strangers" — OK, change it. What the kids who actually use this feature think — doesn't matter.

Your child is the one using the device, not those "parents."

---

## Why This Shows "They Don't Care About Users"

The common thread across these changes:

1. **No advance notice** — the update notes probably didn't even mention it
2. **No choice** — changed is changed, take it or leave it
3. **No compensation** — users' accumulated social assets (like counts, benefit cards) directly depreciated or disappeared

If this were a small company, you could say "they don't know what they're doing." But Xiaotiancai owns such a large share of the children's watch market — and you're telling me they don't know product management or user communication? It's not that they don't know. They don't care.

In their view:
- The real users of the watch are **parents** (the ones who pay)
- Children are just **end users** (no decision-making power)
- So as long as parents don't complain, what kids think doesn't matter

This logic may work in business. It doesn't hold up morally.
